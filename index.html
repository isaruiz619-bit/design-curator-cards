Bauhaus

1919–1933

Bauhaus was a German design movement that focused on combining art, craft, and industry. Its philosophy centered around functionalism — the idea that design should serve a clear purpose. Visually, Bauhaus used primary colors like red, blue, and yellow along with geometric shapes such as circles and squares. The style feels bold but structured. Rather than decorative design, Bauhaus emphasized clarity and usefulness. Its influence can still be seen in modern UI layouts that prioritize simplicity and intentional structure.

Modernism Functionalism Geometric Forms Primary Colors Industrial

Examples: Tate · MoMA: Bauhaus exhibition

Not to be confused with Art Deco, because Bauhaus prioritizes function over decorative ornamentation.

Swiss Style

International Typographic Style · 1950s–1970s

Swiss Style emerged in the 1950s and emphasized clarity, objectivity, and structure. It is known for its strict grid systems, strong typographic hierarchy, and use of sans-serif fonts like Helvetica. Designers prioritized legibility and neutrality over decoration. Layouts were carefully aligned, with generous white space and logical information flow. Many modern websites and app interfaces reflect Swiss principles, especially in structured layouts and clean typography. The movement focused less on expression and more on universal communication.

Grid System Sans-serif Typography Visual Hierarchy Modernism Legibility

Examples: About Switzerland · Smashing Magazine

Not to be confused with Minimalism

New Wave

New Wave Typography · Late 1970s-1980s

New Wave emerged in the late 1970s as a reaction against the strict rules of Swiss Style. While Swiss prioritized grid systems and strict alignment, New Wave designers intentionally disrupted structure. They layered text, played with spacing, and experimented with typographic expression. The movement embraced individuality and visual energy over rigid order. Although sometimes less legible, New Wave challenged the idea that design must always be neutral and structured. It helped shift design into a more expressive, postmodern direction.

Postmodernism Experimental Typography Anti-Grid Expression Visual Disruption

Examples: Eye on Design · Print

Not to be confused with Grunge Typography
